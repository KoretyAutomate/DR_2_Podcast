## Core Concept: 科学は何かを「証明」しない — 不確実性を狭めるだけ

Science does not prove things. It narrows uncertainty. Every scientific finding is a probability statement on a spectrum from "almost certainly true" to "we don't know yet."

### Why This Matters for Working Parents

Parents are bombarded with "scientifically proven" claims every day:
- "This formula is scientifically proven to boost brain development"
- "Studies show screen time causes ADHD"
- "Research proves early education improves test scores"
- "This supplement is clinically proven to improve immunity"

If parents understand that science speaks in probabilities — not certainties — they can:
1. Stop panicking when scientific advice changes ("first eggs were bad, now they're good")
2. See through marketing that uses "proven" as a sales tactic
3. Make better decisions by understanding the confidence level behind each claim
4. Teach their children to think critically

## Key Content to Cover

### 1. The Difference Between Proof and Evidence

**Proof** belongs to mathematics and logic:
- 1 + 1 = 2 — this is proven. It will never change. It's deductive.
- A triangle's angles always sum to 180° — mathematical proof.

**Evidence** belongs to science:
- "Breastfeeding is associated with better cognitive outcomes" — this is evidence, not proof
- "Vaccines reduce the risk of measles by approximately 97%" — strong evidence, but still a probability
- Even gravity is technically a "theory" supported by overwhelming evidence

The key distinction: mathematical proof is absolute and final. Scientific evidence is always provisional — the best explanation we have RIGHT NOW, based on the data collected SO FAR.

### 2. The Probability Spectrum

Introduce the idea that scientific confidence exists on a spectrum:

| Confidence Level | Example | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| ほぼ確実（Almost certain） | 喫煙は肺がんのリスクを高める (Smoking increases lung cancer risk) | Thousands of studies, multiple mechanisms understood, overwhelming consensus |
| 高い確信（High confidence） | 運動は心臓病のリスクを下げる (Exercise reduces heart disease risk) | Strong evidence from many RCTs and cohort studies |
| 中程度の確信（Moderate confidence） | 地中海食は認知機能低下を遅らせる可能性がある (Mediterranean diet may slow cognitive decline) | Several studies suggest this, but not conclusive |
| 低い確信（Low confidence） | ある特定のサプリが免疫力を高める (A specific supplement boosts immunity) | Limited studies, small samples, mixed results |
| まだ分からない（We don't know yet） | 長期的なスマホ使用が子供の脳に与える影響 (Long-term effects of smartphones on children's brains) | Too new, insufficient data |

**Key point:** Most claims parents encounter fall in the "moderate" to "low" range — but marketing presents them as "almost certain."

### 3. Historical Examples of Science Updating Itself

These are NOT failures of science — they're science WORKING:

**胃潰瘍 (Stomach Ulcers):**
- For decades: "Ulcers are caused by stress and spicy food" (high confidence at the time)
- 1982: Barry Marshall discovered H. pylori bacteria cause most ulcers
- He had to drink the bacteria himself because nobody believed him
- Now: antibiotics cure ulcers. The probability shifted dramatically with new evidence.
- **Lesson:** Science didn't "get it wrong" — it updated when better evidence arrived.

**大陸移動説 (Continental Drift):**
- 1912: Alfred Wegener proposed continents move. Ridiculed by the scientific establishment.
- 1960s: Seafloor spreading discovered. Plate tectonics accepted.
- **Lesson:** The probability was low with Wegener's evidence alone. New evidence raised it to near-certainty.

**食事の脂質 (Dietary Fat):**
- 1980s-90s: "All fat is bad. Low-fat diets are healthiest."
- 2000s-present: Saturated fat is more nuanced. Unsaturated fats (olive oil, nuts) are beneficial.
- **Lesson:** The original claim had moderate evidence. More research narrowed the picture.

**Parent-relevant example — うつ伏せ寝 (Prone Sleeping for Infants):**
- Before 1990s: Some doctors recommended babies sleep on their stomachs
- 1990s: "Back to Sleep" campaign after evidence showed prone sleeping increases SIDS risk
- SIDS rates dropped ~50% after the guideline change
- **Lesson:** Science updated based on new evidence, and the update saved lives.

### 4. Everyday Probability Thinking (Parent-Friendly Analogies)

**天気予報 (Weather Forecasts):**
- "明日の降水確率は70%" — nobody panics about this being probabilistic
- You don't say "weather scientists can't make up their minds!"
- You grab an umbrella. You make a decision based on probability.
- Science works the same way. "This treatment works with 70% probability" is useful information.

**子供の発熱 (Child's Fever):**
- Your child has a 38.5°C fever. Is it a cold? Flu? Something serious?
- The doctor says "most likely a viral infection" — probability, not proof
- You don't demand the doctor "prove" the diagnosis before acting
- You accept the probabilistic assessment and follow the recommended course

**健康診断 (Medical Checkups):**
- A cancer screening test comes back positive
- Does this mean you definitely have cancer? No — there are false positives
- The test has a certain sensitivity (true positive rate) and specificity (true negative rate)
- Your actual risk depends on the base rate in your population
- This is Bayesian thinking (preview of Day 13!)

**料理 (Cooking):**
- A recipe says "bake at 180°C for 25-30 minutes"
- The range IS a probability statement — it depends on your oven, the ingredients, the altitude
- You don't say the recipe is "unscientific" because it gives a range instead of an exact number

### 5. Why People Crave Certainty (And Why Science Can't Give It)

**Evolutionary psychology:**
- Our ancestors needed quick, certain decisions: "Is that a predator? YES → run"
- Uncertainty was dangerous. Certainty was survival.
- Our brains are wired to convert "maybe" into "definitely" because hesitation could be fatal

**Modern problem:**
- In complex modern life, most decisions aren't predator-or-not
- "Should I vaccinate my child?" requires probabilistic thinking, not fight-or-flight certainty
- Marketing exploits our certainty bias: "PROVEN to work!" feels more comfortable than "works with moderate probability based on current evidence"

**The discomfort of uncertainty:**
- Parents especially crave certainty — you want to KNOW what's best for your child
- "It might help" feels unsatisfying compared to "it definitely works"
- But embracing probability actually gives you MORE control: you can make informed bets rather than being manipulated by false certainties

### 6. The Scientific Method as a Probability-Narrowing Machine

Walk through the scientific method — but frame it as a process that narrows probability, not one that finds "truth":

1. **観察 (Observation):** "Children who read more seem to score higher on tests"
   - Probability: ??? (we don't know direction yet)

2. **仮説 (Hypothesis):** "Reading 30 minutes daily improves test scores"
   - Probability: Still unknown, but now testable

3. **実験 (Experiment):** Randomly assign 200 children: half read 30 min/day, half don't. Test after 6 months.
   - Probability: Starting to narrow

4. **分析 (Analysis):** Reading group scored 15% higher (p = 0.01, 95% CI: 8%-22%)
   - Probability: Now we can say "likely between 8% and 22% improvement"

5. **再現 (Replication):** Three more teams repeat the experiment with similar results
   - Probability: Narrowing further — high confidence

6. **メタ分析 (Meta-analysis):** Combine all 4 studies: pooled effect = 13% improvement (95% CI: 10%-16%)
   - Probability: Very narrow range now — strong evidence

**Key insight:** At no point did we "prove" that reading improves test scores. We narrowed the probability from "no idea" to "very likely, with an effect size of about 10-16%." That's incredibly useful — and it's honest.

## Common Misconceptions to Address

### 「科学が変わるなら信用できない」(If science changes, we can't trust it)

**Wrong framing.** Science changing IS science working. It would be terrifying if science NEVER changed — that would mean we stopped learning.

**Better framing:** Science is like a GPS recalculating. When you take a wrong turn, the GPS updates. You don't throw away the GPS because it recalculated — you trust it MORE because it adapts.

### 「この研究が証明した」(This study proved it)

**Wrong.** A single study provides evidence. It shifts the probability. It does NOT prove anything.

**Better:** "This study found evidence consistent with X" or "This study increases our confidence that X is likely true."

### 「科学者は意見が一致していない」(Scientists can't agree)

**Misleading.** On most established topics, there IS strong consensus (vaccines, climate change, evolution). Media amplifies the 3% who disagree because conflict makes better headlines.

**Better:** "The overwhelming consensus among researchers is X, though some details are still being refined."

### 「自然なものは安全」(Natural things are safe)

This is a logical fallacy (preview of Day 8), but it's rooted in the certainty bias. People want a simple rule: natural = good, artificial = bad.

**Reality:** Arsenic is natural. Poison ivy is natural. The probability of safety depends on the substance and the dose, not whether it's "natural."

## Real-World Application: The Probability Lens

**Starting today, practice asking yourself:**

1. When you see "scientifically proven" → ask "How confident? What's the probability level?"
2. When science "changes its mind" → ask "What new evidence narrowed the probability?"
3. When a claim seems certain → ask "Is this actually high-probability, or is someone selling me certainty?"
4. When making a parenting decision → ask "What does the weight of evidence suggest?" not "What's proven?"

**Example for parents:**
- Headline: 「早期英語教育は子供の言語発達に効果がある」(Early English education benefits children's language development)
- Old reaction: "Great, it's proven! Sign my kid up!"
- New reaction (after today): "How confident are we? What kind of studies? How large was the effect? What's the probability this applies to MY child's situation?"

## Key Terms to Define (First Use)

| Term | Explanation |
|---|---|
| エビデンス (Evidence) | 科学的な証拠。研究や実験から得られたデータに基づく根拠のこと |
| 確率 (Probability) | ある事象が起こる可能性の度合い。0%（絶対起こらない）から100%（絶対起こる）の間で表される |
| 仮説 (Hypothesis) | まだ検証されていない予測。「こうではないか」という科学的な問いかけ |
| 再現性 (Replication) | 同じ実験を別の研究者が繰り返して同じ結果が出ること。科学の信頼性の柱 |
| メタ分析 (Meta-analysis) | 複数の研究結果を統合して分析する手法。個々の研究より信頼性が高い（Day 2で詳しく） |
| RCT（ランダム化比較試験） | 参加者をランダムに2グループに分け、効果を比較する実験デザイン（Day 3で詳しく） |
