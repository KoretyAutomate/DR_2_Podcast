**Central Thesis:** 科学＝確率 (Science = Probability, Not Proof)

## Series Narrative Arc

The 14 episodes follow a deliberate persuasion arc:

1. **Days 1-7 (Week 1):** "How Science Generates Knowledge"
   - Listeners learn the mechanics — what good evidence looks like, how science produces it
   - The "Science = Probability" thesis is established on Day 1 and reinforced in every episode

2. **Days 8-12 (Week 2, Mon-Fri):** "Why Evaluating Evidence Is So Hard"
   - Internal threats (biases, fallacies) → external threats (COI, marketing, bad media, weak journals)
   - Listeners realize the system is full of traps

3. **Days 13-14 (Week 2, Sat-Sun):** "How to Think and Act"
   - Day 13: The master framework for updating beliefs (Bayesian thinking)
   - Day 14: A full worked example that reveals the enormous effort required — listeners naturally conclude they need a trusted source

**The arc builds to Day 14's implicit conclusion without ever stating it directly.**

## Background: Why "Science = Probability" Must Come First

Most people think science "proves" things. This misconception is the root of:
- Shock when scientific recommendations change ("first eggs were bad, now they're good")
- Vulnerability to marketing ("clinically proven!")
- Distrust of science when findings conflict ("scientists can't agree on anything")
- Black-and-white thinking ("is coffee good or bad?")

If listeners understand from Day 1 that every scientific finding is a probability statement on a spectrum of certainty, they can:
- Accept that science updates itself (narrowing probability, not flip-flopping)
- See through absolute marketing claims (nothing is "proven")
- Evaluate conflicting studies (different probability estimates, not contradictions)
- Understand why meta-analyses matter (aggregating probability estimates)

This is why the thesis must be introduced on Day 1 and reinforced in EVERY subsequent episode.

## Episode Details

### Day 1: Science = Probability, Not Proof

**Core Concept:** Science doesn't prove anything — it narrows uncertainty. Every scientific finding is a probability statement, not a fact.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand that "proof" belongs to math and logic, not empirical science
- See scientific findings as points on a probability spectrum
- Recognize that science "changing its mind" is science working correctly

**Key Content:**
- The difference between mathematical proof and empirical evidence
- The probability spectrum: from "almost certainly true" (gravity) to "we don't know yet" (dark matter)
- Historical examples of science updating itself:
  - Stomach ulcers: "caused by stress" → caused by H. pylori bacteria (Barry Marshall, 1982)
  - Continental drift: ridiculed for 50 years → plate tectonics accepted
  - Dietary fat: "all fat is bad" → nuanced view (saturated vs. unsaturated)
- Everyday probability thinking: weather forecasts, medical screening, sports predictions
- Why people crave certainty and why science can't give it
- The scientific method as a probability-narrowing machine: hypothesis → test → update probability

**Common Misconceptions Addressed:**
- "Scientists proved X" — no, they found strong evidence supporting X
- "The study shows Y" — the study found results consistent with Y
- "Science keeps changing, so we can't trust it" — that's exactly how it works

**Callback:** This IS the thesis. Established here, reinforced in every subsequent episode.

### Day 2: The Hierarchy of Evidence — Not All Evidence Is Equal

**Core Concept:** Evidence exists on a spectrum of reliability. A friend's anecdote and a 50,000-person clinical trial are both "evidence" — but they carry vastly different weight.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Memorize the evidence hierarchy (anecdote → case report → observational → RCT → systematic review → meta-analysis)
- Understand WHY higher levels are more reliable (more controls, less bias)
- Recognize where common claims fall on the hierarchy

**Key Content:**
- The pyramid: anecdotes at the bottom, meta-analyses at the top
- Each level explained with a relatable example (e.g., "my uncle smoked and lived to 95" vs. a 50-year cohort study of 40,000 people)
- Why anecdotes are compelling but unreliable (small sample, no controls, cherry-picked)
- Why RCTs are powerful (randomization eliminates confounders)
- Why meta-analyses sit at the top (aggregate many studies, reduce individual study quirks)
- Expert opinion: where it fits (informed by evidence, but still one person's interpretation)

**Real-World Application:**
- When a friend says "X worked for me" — where does this sit on the hierarchy?
- When a news article says "a study found" — what kind of study?
- When a product claims "clinically tested" — what does that actually mean?

**Callback:** "Higher-quality evidence = narrower probability range. A meta-analysis gives you a tighter probability estimate than any single study."

### Day 3: Study Designs — How Evidence Is Built

**Core Concept:** Different study designs answer different questions with different levels of certainty. Understanding design is the first step to evaluating any study.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Distinguish observational from experimental studies
- Understand cross-sectional, cohort, case-control, and RCT designs
- Know what each design CAN and CANNOT tell you

**Key Content:**
- **Cross-sectional:** Snapshot in time. "People who exercise report less depression." But which came first?
- **Cohort:** Follow people forward. "We tracked 10,000 people for 20 years." Stronger but still observational.
- **Case-control:** Look backward. "People with lung cancer smoked more." Useful but prone to recall bias.
- **RCT (Randomized Controlled Trial):** The gold standard. Random assignment eliminates confounders. But expensive, time-consuming, sometimes unethical.
- **Why we can't always do RCTs:** You can't randomly assign people to smoke for 30 years. Ethics limit what we can test experimentally.
- **Natural experiments:** When nature or policy creates quasi-random groups (e.g., a city banning smoking in restaurants)

**Real-World Application:**
- Reading a health headline: "Coffee drinkers live longer" — what design would tell us if coffee CAUSES longer life?
- Why "correlation studies" dominate health news (cheaper, faster, but weaker)

**Callback:** "Each study design controls different sources of uncertainty. Better design = tighter probability estimate."

### Day 4: Correlation vs. Causation — The Most Common Mistake

**Core Concept:** Just because two things happen together doesn't mean one causes the other. This is the single most common error in interpreting science.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Define correlation and causation precisely
- Identify three alternative explanations: reverse causation, confounding, coincidence
- Apply the Bradford Hill criteria (simplified) to evaluate causal claims

**Key Content:**
- Classic examples: ice cream sales and drowning deaths (both caused by summer heat)
- Reverse causation: "Depressed people watch more TV." Does TV cause depression, or does depression cause TV-watching?
- Confounders: "Wine drinkers are healthier" — wine drinkers also tend to be wealthier, eat better, exercise more
- The Texas Sharpshooter fallacy: drawing the target after shooting
- Spurious correlations: Nicolas Cage movies and pool drownings
- Bradford Hill criteria (simplified): strength, consistency, temporality, biological plausibility, dose-response
- Why establishing causation requires RCTs or very strong observational evidence from multiple angles

**Real-World Application:**
- Supplement marketing: "People who take Vitamin X have lower rates of Y" — correlation or causation?
- News headlines: how to mentally flag "correlation, not causation" when reading

**Callback:** "Correlation gives you the probability of an association, not the probability of causation. That's a crucial difference."

### Day 5: Reading a Study — Design, Sample & Methodology

**Core Concept:** You don't need a PhD to evaluate a study. A few key questions about design and methodology tell you most of what you need to know.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Know what sample size means and why it matters
- Understand control groups, randomization, and blinding
- Identify red flags: no control group, self-selected participants, tiny sample

**Key Content:**
- **Sample size:** Why 12 people isn't enough. The law of large numbers. When is "large enough" large enough?
- **Control groups:** Why you need a comparison. The placebo effect is real and powerful.
- **Randomization:** Why random assignment is magic — it balances all confounders, even ones you don't know about
- **Blinding:** Single-blind (participant doesn't know), double-blind (researcher doesn't know either). Why blinding prevents bias.
- **Inclusion/exclusion criteria:** Who was in the study? Young, healthy college students? Or the actual population the claim is about?
- **Red flags checklist:**
  - No control group
  - Self-selected participants (volunteers only)
  - Funded by the company selling the product
  - Tiny sample (n < 30)
  - No blinding
  - Unrealistic dropout rate

**Real-World Application:**
- A supplement ad says "In a clinical trial, 80% of participants felt better." Questions: How many participants? Was there a placebo group? Who funded it?

**Callback:** "Small sample = wide probability range. When someone cites a study of 12 people, your uncertainty should stay high."

### Day 6: Reading a Study — P-values, Effect Size & Confidence Intervals

**Core Concept:** The numbers in a study tell a story — but only if you know how to read them. P-values, effect sizes, and confidence intervals are the language of scientific probability.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand what a p-value actually means (and what it doesn't)
- Distinguish statistical significance from practical significance
- Read a confidence interval as a probability range

**Key Content:**
- **P-value demystified:** "If there were truly no effect, how likely is it we'd see results this extreme?" That's it. Not "the probability the result is true."
- **The p < 0.05 convention:** Why 5%? It's arbitrary! Fisher suggested it as a rough guide, not a sacred threshold.
- **P-hacking:** Running many tests until one hits p < 0.05 by chance. If you test 20 things, one will be "significant" by accident.
- **Effect size:** HOW BIG is the effect? A statistically significant but tiny effect may not matter in practice.
  - Example: A drug that lowers blood pressure by 0.5 mmHg with p = 0.001. Statistically significant, practically meaningless.
- **Confidence intervals:** "We're 95% confident the true effect is between X and Y." Wide interval = high uncertainty. Narrow interval = more precise.
- **Statistical significance vs. practical significance:** A study can be statistically significant but clinically meaningless, or statistically non-significant but practically important (underpowered study).

**Real-World Application:**
- "A study found a statistically significant effect (p = 0.03)." Is this impressive? You need to know the effect size and confidence interval too.

**Callback:** "A p-value IS a probability statement. A confidence interval IS a probability range. This is science = probability in its purest form."

### Day 7: Cognitive Biases — How Your Brain Betrays Probability

**Core Concept:** The human brain evolved for quick decisions, not accurate probability estimation. Understanding your cognitive biases is the first step to thinking clearly about evidence.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Identify the 5 most relevant biases for science evaluation
- Recognize when you're falling into a bias trap
- Develop mental "circuit breakers" for each bias

**Key Content:**
- **Confirmation bias:** You seek and remember evidence that supports what you already believe. You ignore or dismiss contradicting evidence.
  - Example: You believe organic food is healthier. You remember every study supporting this and forget every one that doesn't.
- **Survivorship bias:** You see the winners, not the losers. "My grandpa smoked and lived to 90" — you don't hear from the smokers who died at 50.
- **Availability heuristic:** You judge probability by how easily examples come to mind. Shark attacks feel common because they make the news. Falls from beds kill far more people.
- **Anchoring:** The first number you hear becomes your reference point. "Originally $200, now $50!" — the $200 anchors your perception of value.
- **Dunning-Kruger effect:** The less you know about a topic, the more confident you tend to be. True expertise brings awareness of what you don't know.
- **Circuit breakers:** For each bias, a practical question to ask yourself:
  - Confirmation: "What would change my mind?"
  - Survivorship: "Who am I NOT hearing from?"
  - Availability: "Is this actually common, or just memorable?"
  - Anchoring: "What does the evidence say independent of that first number?"
  - Dunning-Kruger: "How much have I actually studied this?"

**Callback:** "Your brain is wired to convert probability into false certainty. Every bias we discussed today is your mind fighting against probabilistic thinking."

### Day 8: Logical Fallacies in Science Debates

**Core Concept:** Flawed arguments can sound convincing even when the underlying logic is broken. Recognizing common fallacies is a critical defense against misinformation.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Identify 6 common fallacies in health/science discussions
- Understand why each fallacy is logically invalid
- Spot them in real-world debates, ads, and social media

**Key Content:**
- **Appeal to authority:** "Dr. X says it works, so it must." Credentials don't equal evidence. Even experts can be wrong or conflicted.
- **Appeal to nature:** "It's natural, so it's safe/better." Arsenic, poison ivy, and earthquakes are natural too.
- **Straw man:** Misrepresenting an argument to make it easier to attack. "You think we should eat more vegetables? So you want to ban meat?"
- **False dichotomy:** Presenting only two options when more exist. "Either this supplement works or doctors are lying to you."
- **Cherry-picking:** Selecting only favorable evidence while ignoring unfavorable. "3 out of 100 studies support my claim — look at these 3!"
- **Ad hominem:** Attacking the person instead of the argument. "You're not a doctor, so your analysis of this study is invalid."
- **Bonus — Anecdotal fallacy:** "My experience proves it works" — one data point vs. systematic evidence.

**Real-World Application:**
- Walk through a real supplement ad or health debate and identify every fallacy present
- Social media: "This doctor says X" — is this authority or evidence?

**Callback:** "Logical fallacies are shortcuts that disguise weak probability as certainty. 'It's natural, so it must be safe' skips the probability question entirely."

### Day 9: Conflict of Interest — Who Paid for This Study?

**Core Concept:** The people funding and conducting research have interests that can — consciously or unconsciously — skew results. Understanding conflict of interest is essential to evaluating any evidence.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand how funding bias works mechanistically
- Know where to find conflict-of-interest disclosures
- Apply a "follow the money" lens to any study or claim

**Key Content:**
- **The funding effect:** Industry-funded studies are significantly more likely to find favorable results. This is not a conspiracy — it's study design choices.
- **How COI warps design (not just results):**
  - Choosing a weak comparator (compare your drug to placebo, not to the best existing treatment)
  - Setting the wrong endpoint (measure a blood marker instead of actual health outcomes)
  - Stopping the trial early when results look good (before they might regress to the mean)
  - Selective outcome reporting (run 20 endpoints, report the 2 that worked)
  - Publication bias (file-drawer problem: negative results never get published)
- **Historical case studies:**
  - Tobacco industry: funded "research" for decades to cast doubt on smoking-cancer link
  - Sugar industry: paid Harvard researchers in the 1960s to blame fat instead of sugar for heart disease
  - Pharmaceutical industry: ghostwriting of medical papers
- **Where to check COI:**
  - Disclosure statements at the end of papers
  - ClinicalTrials.gov (who registered and funded the trial)
  - Dollars for Docs (industry payments to physicians)
- **Principal Investigator (PI) conflicts:**
  - Consulting fees, speaking fees, stock ownership
  - Academic pressure to publish positive results
  - Career incentives that reward novelty over replication

**Real-World Application:**
- When evaluating any claim: "Who funded this research? What do they stand to gain?"
- How to quickly check a study's funding and COI disclosures

**Callback:** "Conflicts of interest don't just bias results — they skew the probabilities you're shown. Funded studies systematically shift the numbers."

### Day 10: "Clinically Proven" — How Marketing Weaponizes Evidence

**Core Concept:** Companies use scientific-sounding language to sell products. Knowing how to decode marketing claims is a practical survival skill in a world full of "evidence-based" products.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Decode common marketing phrases ("clinically proven," "doctor recommended," "scientifically formulated")
- Understand how companies cherry-pick evidence
- Develop a quick evaluation framework for product claims

**Key Content:**
- **Decoding marketing language:**
  - "Clinically proven" — proven by whom? What kind of clinical trial? Often means "we did one small, uncontrolled study"
  - "Doctor recommended" — which doctors? How many? Paid?
  - "Scientifically formulated" — meaningless. Water is scientifically formulated (H2O).
  - "Studies show" — which studies? How many? What quality?
  - "Up to X% improvement" — "up to" means the best-case scenario, not the average
  - "Backed by research" — one pilot study with 15 participants counts as "research"
- **The cherry-picking playbook:**
  - Fund 20 studies, 1 finds a positive result, market only that one
  - Use surrogate endpoints ("improves blood marker X" ≠ "improves health")
  - Compare to no treatment instead of existing best treatment
  - Use relative risk instead of absolute risk ("50% reduction!" — from 2 in 10,000 to 1 in 10,000)
- **Real product claim teardowns:**
  - Walk through 2-3 real supplement/health product claims
  - Trace each claim back to its source study
  - Evaluate the study quality using Days 1-9 knowledge
- **The "3 questions" quick test:**
  1. What specific study are they citing? (If none, red flag)
  2. Who funded the study? (If the company itself, yellow flag)
  3. What was the study design and sample size? (If weak, red flag)

**Callback:** "Marketing sells you certainty manufactured from thin probability. 'Clinically proven' is a certainty claim attached to a probability finding."

### Day 11: Peer Review, Predatory Journals & the Replication Crisis

**Core Concept:** "Published in a peer-reviewed journal" sounds authoritative — but the quality filter is imperfect, and the system has serious holes.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand how peer review actually works (and its limitations)
- Recognize predatory journals
- Understand the replication crisis and why it matters

**Key Content:**
- **How peer review works:**
  - Author submits → editor screens → 2-3 reviewers evaluate → revise → publish (or reject)
  - Reviewers are volunteers, unpaid, doing this alongside their own work
  - Process takes months to years
- **What peer review catches:** Major methodological flaws, unsupported conclusions, missing references
- **What peer review misses:** Subtle statistical manipulation, fraud, p-hacking, selective reporting. Reviewers rarely re-analyze raw data.
- **Predatory journals:**
  - Pay-to-publish journals with fake or non-existent peer review
  - How to spot them: unsolicited emails, rapid "acceptance," no editorial board of substance
  - Beall's List and Think.Check.Submit resources
  - Why they matter: anyone can publish anything and call it "peer-reviewed"
- **The Replication Crisis:**
  - 2015 Open Science Collaboration: only 36% of psychology studies replicated
  - Similar issues in medicine, biology, economics
  - Why: publication bias (positive results published, negative results filed away), p-hacking, small samples, pressure to publish novel findings
  - What's being done: pre-registration, open data, registered reports
- **Impact journals vs. predatory journals:** Not all journals are equal. Impact factor, citation metrics, and editorial reputation matter.

**Callback:** "Published doesn't mean proven. Many published findings don't replicate. The probability estimate they reported may be wrong."

### Day 12: How Media Distorts Science

**Core Concept:** The journey from a scientific paper to a news headline involves multiple layers of distortion. Understanding this chain helps you trace claims back to their source.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Map the distortion chain: paper → press release → journalist → headline → social media
- Identify 5 common media distortion patterns
- Practice tracing a headline back to its source

**Key Content:**
- **The distortion chain:**
  1. Scientist writes paper with careful caveats
  2. University press office writes press release (caveats removed, findings hyped)
  3. Journalist writes article from press release (often without reading the paper)
  4. Editor writes headline (clickbait, absolute claims)
  5. Social media shares headline only (all context lost)
- **Common distortion patterns:**
  - "Breakthrough!" — incremental finding in mice hyped as a cure
  - Relative vs. absolute risk — "50% increase!" sounds terrifying; absolute increase from 1 in 100,000 to 1.5 in 100,000 doesn't
  - Animal studies reported as human implications — "Compound X shrinks tumors" (in mice)
  - Preliminary = definitive — "New research suggests" becomes "Scientists discover"
  - Causation from correlation — "Coffee linked to longer life" becomes "Drink coffee to live longer"
- **How to trace back:**
  1. Find the actual study (not just the article)
  2. Read the abstract and conclusions (not just the title)
  3. Check the sample: humans or animals? How many? What population?
  4. Check who funded it
  5. Look for caveats in the discussion section
- **Practice exercise:** Take a recent health headline and walk through the entire trace-back process live

**Callback:** "Media turns probability into certainty for clicks. A '30% increased risk' headline erases all the uncertainty from the original study."

### Day 13: Bayesian Thinking — How to Update Your Beliefs

**Core Concept:** Bayesian thinking is the master framework for rational evidence evaluation. You start with what you believe, encounter new evidence, and update proportionally.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand Bayes' theorem intuitively (no math required)
- Apply Bayesian updating to everyday claims
- Know when extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence

**Key Content:**
- **The intuitive version:**
  - You start with a **prior** (what you believe before seeing new evidence)
  - You encounter **new evidence** (a study, an observation, a claim)
  - You calculate a **posterior** (your updated belief)
  - The strength of the update depends on the quality and surprise of the evidence
- **The key insight:** Strong priors need strong evidence to overturn
  - If 100 studies say X works, one study saying it doesn't shouldn't flip your belief
  - If we have no idea whether X works, even a single good study can shift you significantly
- **Extraordinary claims need extraordinary evidence:**
  - "This supplement slightly improves energy" — modest claim, modest evidence needed
  - "This supplement cures cancer" — extraordinary claim, extraordinary evidence needed
  - Why? Because your prior for "supplements cure cancer" should be very low
- **Everyday Bayesian thinking:**
  - Medical screening: why a positive test doesn't mean you definitely have the disease (base rate matters)
  - "My friend tried X and it worked" — how much should this update your belief? (Very little — low-quality evidence against a neutral prior)
  - Multiple studies disagreeing — how to weight them (larger, better-designed studies get more weight in your update)
- **The probability spectrum revisited:**
  - After Bayesian updating, you arrive at a POSITION on the probability spectrum
  - You NEVER arrive at certainty — just higher or lower confidence
  - This is exactly what we said on Day 1: science = probability

**Callback:** "Bayesian thinking IS thinking in probabilities. You start with a prior, update with evidence, arrive at a new probability — never certainty."

### Day 14: Putting It All Together — The Real Cost of Knowing

**Core Concept:** Properly evaluating a single claim requires applying everything from Days 1-13. This episode demonstrates the full process — and reveals its true cost.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Apply the complete evidence evaluation framework to one real claim
- Experience the full complexity of proper evaluation
- Arrive at a nuanced probability statement, not a yes/no answer

**Key Content:**
- **The claim:** "Vitamin D supplementation prevents cancer" (or similar widely-discussed claim)
- **Step-by-step walkthrough:**
  1. **Set the prior (Day 13):** What do we believe before looking at evidence? Vitamin D is biologically plausible for cancer prevention (receptors in many tissues). Moderate prior.
  2. **Find all relevant studies (Day 2, 3):** Search PubMed, Cochrane, Google Scholar. Find RCTs, meta-analyses, observational studies. How many exist? What types?
  3. **Evaluate each study's design (Day 3, 5):** Were the RCTs well-designed? What were the sample sizes? Was there proper blinding?
  4. **Check the numbers (Day 6):** What were the effect sizes? P-values? Confidence intervals? Were effects clinically meaningful or just statistically significant?
  5. **Check who funded it (Day 9):** Industry-funded studies vs. independent research. Any PI conflicts?
  6. **Look for replication (Day 11):** Do independent studies find the same thing? Or is it one positive study among many nulls?
  7. **Check for media distortion (Day 12):** How are these studies being reported? Is the media overselling or underselling?
  8. **Identify biases and fallacies (Day 7, 8):** Are we falling for confirmation bias? Is the cherry-picking fallacy at play?
  9. **Synthesize with Bayesian updating (Day 13):** Given ALL the evidence, where do we land on the probability spectrum?
  10. **Arrive at a probability statement:** "Based on current evidence, Vitamin D supplementation has a [low/moderate/unclear] probability of reducing cancer risk, with effects — if real — likely small. The evidence is [mixed/promising but insufficient/moderate]. More large-scale RCTs are needed."
- **The reveal:** This process took significant effort for ONE claim. Now imagine doing this for every health headline, product claim, or news story you encounter daily.
- **The framework summary:**
  - Source → Study Design → Sample → Effect Size → Funding/COI → Replication → Media Filter → Bayesian Update → Probability Statement
- **Closing:** "You now have the tools to evaluate evidence. You understand what good science looks like, how it can be manipulated, and how hard it is to do this properly. The question is — how will you use this going forward?"

**Callback:** "Every claim falls somewhere on the probability spectrum. The question isn't 'is it true?' but 'how confident should I be?'"

### Dependencies Between Episodes
